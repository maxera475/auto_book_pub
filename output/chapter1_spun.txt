CHAPTER I: THE CANOE BUILDER

Dick stood at the edge of the coral ledge, his gaze fixed on the southern horizon. Behind him, the mighty waves of the outer sea crashed against the shore, sending spindrift flying through the air. Before him lay the tranquil lagoon of Karolin, a crystal-clear expanse that stretched out to infinity.

The warm sunlight danced across the water, casting a shimmering glow on the fishing gulls that soared overhead. The tribe was busy along the beach, their nets and games forgotten in the excitement of the day. Women, children, youths – all were engaged in their daily routines, unaware of the stranger who had arrived only yesterday.

As Dick's eyes scanned the horizon, his brow furrowed with concern. He turned his back to the lagoon and faced the breakers on the outer beach, his gaze fixed on the northern sea. Beyond that line lay Palm Tree Island, a place where nightmares lurked in the shadows. Little did he know that his presence here was not just a coincidence – it was a calling from the gods themselves.

Sitting nearby on the coral ledge was Tari, a young boy who knew nothing of life beyond Karolin's shores. His eyes were bright with curiosity as he gazed at Dick. "Who are you?" the child asked, his voice innocent and unfiltered.

Dick hesitated, lost in thought. He could have shared the story of his past, but it was shrouded in mystery. The memories of his childhood on Marua Island had long since faded, replaced by the harsh realities of survival. All that remained were fragmented images – a ship burning, Katafa's laughter, and the feeling of being adrift.

As he looked down at Tari, Dick's eyes clouded with uncertainty. "I am Taori," he said finally, his voice low and gravelly. The child's eyes sparkled with understanding, but only for a moment. His attention was soon diverted by the commotion on the beach.

Dick's gaze drifted back to the northern sea, where Palm Tree Island loomed in the distance. Memories of the Melanesian invaders flooded his mind – their cruel laughter, the burning ship, and the desperate flight from the island in a dinghy. He knew that they would return, driven by a hunger for destruction.

The sound of Katafa's hand on his shoulder broke the spell. Together, they stood transfixed, watching as Palm Tree Island began to materialize on the horizon. The island rose like a mirage, its beauty twisted and tainted by the horrors of their past.

As the vision solidified, Dick's nostrils flared with rage. They had threatened him, but it was Katafa who had suffered most at their hands. The memory still lingered, a festering wound that would never fully heal.

Somehow, he knew that they would come again – driven by their baser instincts and fueled by greed. The island of Palm Tree loomed before them, a constant reminder of the dangers that lurked beyond the horizon.

As they turned away from the sea, Katafa pointed to a canoe approaching from the southern beach. It was the vessel Dick had sent for the old canoe builders – Aioma, Palia, and Tafata. The lagoon water sparkled in the sunlight as the canoe drew closer, its occupants eager to begin their new life on Karolin's shores.
